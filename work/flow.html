Flow & RFI Builder

A web platform that replaced Excel-and-email chaos with a centralized RFI system — turning a multi-week process into a few days for the world's largest brands and their retail partners.

The problem

An annual evaluation. Over 200 questions. Managed entirely by email.

Every year, The Coca-Cola Company evaluated its retail media partners — Walmart, Amazon, Kroger, Target, Safeway, and dozens more — to decide where to invest its retail advertising budget. The evaluation was rigorous: over 200 weighted questions across nine media channels, from on-property search to programmatic display to connected TV. The data directly shaped Coca-Cola's media plans for the second half of the year.

For years, this evaluation lived in an Excel spreadsheet.

GroupM's commerce team emailed the workbook to each partner retailer. Retailers filled it out at their own pace. Responses came back over email — sometimes complete, sometimes partial, sometimes lost in someone's inbox. The team manually tracked who had responded, chased the laggards, parsed the data by hand, and stitched together a report for Coca-Cola.

For a single client with over 50 retail partners, a complete cycle could stretch into weeks. And Coca-Cola wasn't the only client running these evaluations.

GroupM needed a centralized platform that could automate the workflow end-to-end: build an RFI once, send it to the right retailers, track responses in real time, and feed the data directly into Fusion for analysis. That platform became Flow.

Key terms
RFI Request for Information. A structured questionnaire built around a specific commerce capability or use case. Different clients can use different RFIs depending on their priorities.
Flow A specific RFI sent to a group of partner retailers on behalf of a client. Think of an RFI as the template — a Flow is the active campaign that uses it.
Partner retailers The retailers a brand sells through (Walmart, Amazon, Kroger, Target, Safeway, etc.) — the audience receiving and filling out the RFI.
Fusion GroupM's commerce intelligence platform where RFI response data was published for analysis. Flow fed data into Fusion; the two platforms worked in tandem.
My role

Sole designer on a four-person product team

I was the sole designer on a small product team — one PM, two engineers, and me — building Flow from scratch. I owned design end-to-end: research, information architecture, wireframes, hi-fi mockups, and the clickable prototype that went into engineering's hands.

Working on a four-person team meant design decisions were made in real time alongside engineers. Tradeoffs that would normally take weeks to negotiate across siloed teams happened in the same standup. The constraint kept me close to what we could actually ship in our four-month MVP timeline — and it shaped multiple decisions you'll see below.

Discovery — Defining the requirements

What the commerce team actually needed

Before sketching screens, I needed to understand exactly what the commerce team's workflow looked like, what was breaking, and what couldn't change. I spent the early weeks of the project in working sessions with the commerce team — the people who'd been running the Excel-based RFI process for years.

A few things came out of those conversations that shaped the entire product:

Finding 01

The RFI was complex — and growing

Coca-Cola's Retail Capabilities Evaluation spanned over 200 weighted questions across nine media channels: search, display, social, CTV, audio, OOH, and API access. Whatever we built had to handle that level of structural complexity and accommodate clients adding new channels year over year. A rigid form wouldn't work.

Finding 02

Tracking was the real pain point

The single biggest complaint about the Excel process wasn't the form itself — it was the chaos of trying to figure out which of 50+ retailers had responded, which were partial, which were late. Status visibility had to be a first-class feature, not an afterthought.

Finding 03

Partner retailers had no system to learn

Whatever the retailer-facing experience looked like, it needed to be self-explanatory on first contact. Retailers weren't going to watch a training video before filling out a Coca-Cola RFI. The experience had to be immediately intuitive.

Build vs. buy — The pivotal solution decision

Automate the wrapper, or replace the process entirely?

Two viable approaches emerged for how Flow could replace the Excel workflow.

Approach Option 1 — Automate Excel Option 2 — Web-based RFI
What it does
Auto-send the existing Excel RFI to retailers, give them a link to upload completed responses, parse data back into Fusion on submission.
Replace Excel entirely. Retailers fill out a structured web form; data lives in the platform from the moment of submission.
Design effort
Low — preserves existing retailer behavior
High — new interface, new interaction patterns
Key risk
Parsing inconsistent retailer-uploaded Excel files reliably is its own backend problem — too unpredictable to ship in our window
More upfront work, but unlocks structured data, real-time tracking, and a scalable path forward
Decision
—
✓ We chose this

The bet was that the upfront design investment would pay off across every future client and every future RFI. The data parsing problem with Option 1 was too unpredictable to ship reliably in our four-month window — and it wouldn't have given us the real-time tracking the commerce team needed most.

Designing Flow creation

Making complexity feel manageable

Creating a new Flow turned out to be more complex than the Excel process ever was — because we were now letting admins do four things that the spreadsheet workflow had bundled together informally: choose what RFI to use, decide which partner retailers receive it, set who can administer the Flow inside GroupM, and review before sending.

An earlier wireframe pass put all four on a single long-form page. It worked technically, but it overwhelmed users — there was no sense of progress and too many decisions visible at once.

I broke it into a stepper inside a modal:

Step 01
Select RFI Type
Choose an existing RFI template or create a new one from scratch
Step 02
Select Partners
Add the partner retailers receiving this Flow and assign admin access
Step 03
RFI Builder
Build or review the question structure for this Flow
Step 04
Review & Send
Final confirmation before the Flow is sent to all selected partners

The stepper handled the complexity. The modal handled the focus — when you're creating a Flow, you shouldn't be looking at twelve other dashboard elements at the same time.

This pattern became the foundation for every multi-step creation flow in the product. Cloning an RFI, editing partner lists, building a new RFI from scratch — all reused the same component, which kept the experience consistent and saved engineering time on every subsequent feature.

The RFI Builder

A form-builder that thinks like a spreadsheet

This screen did the most heavy lifting in the entire product. It had to be flexible enough to capture the 200+ weighted questions of Coca-Cola's Retail Capabilities Evaluation, simple enough that a commerce strategist could build a new RFI without engineering help, and structured enough that the captured data could feed directly into Fusion for analysis.

I designed it as a table.

Add screenshot here — RFI Builder (Step 3 of Create Flow stepper), showing table with Channel, Category, Question, and Partner Input Type columns

Each row is a single question. Columns capture the structural metadata the data team needed: Channel (CTV, Audio, Offsite Display, etc.), Category (Buying Model, Measurement, Depth of Offering), the question itself, and the Partner Input Type (Freeform, Single Select, Multi Select). An Add Row / Add Column control lets admins extend it as needed.

Decision 01

Treating the form like data, not like a form

Most form-builders mimic the look of the form being built. This one looks like a spreadsheet — because that's the mental model commerce strategists already had from years of doing the work in Excel. Keeping the table metaphor reduced the cognitive cost of learning the new tool, even though the underlying behavior was completely different.

Decision 02

Question types as a column, not separate flows

The team's first instinct was to create separate "Add a Single Select Question" and "Add a Multi Select Question" flows. Putting input type as a dropdown column meant any question could be changed at any time without rebuilding it — and kept the table scannable.

Decision 03

Inline expansion for type-specific config

When Multi Select is chosen, the row expands inline to reveal a label/option editor with draggable chips. Other types stay collapsed. This kept the default table dense and readable, while still allowing the depth that complex question types required.

Post-MVP, Clone Existing RFI became a top-requested feature. Once one Coca-Cola RFI was built well, it could become the starting point for Nestle and Unilever — cutting setup time from hours to minutes.
Managing Flows — The homepage

The operational hub for the commerce team

Once Flows existed, admins needed a place to manage them. The Flow homepage became the operational hub where the commerce team tracked everything running in parallel.

Add screenshot here — Flow homepage showing RFI groups (Retail Media Capabilities, DSP Capabilities) with Flow cards and progress bars
IA Decision

Grouped by RFI type, not by client

Early sketches grouped Flows by client — all Coca-Cola Flows together, all Nestle Flows together. User testing flipped that. Commerce admins were more often working across clients on the same capability than across capabilities for one client. Grouping by RFI type mapped to how they actually thought about their week.

Card Design

Triage at a glance

Each Flow card surfaces what an admin needs to triage quickly: the Flow name, who created it, when it was last touched, and a progress bar showing how close it is to completion. The progress bar is the single most-used affordance on the screen — more on how it got there in Section 9.

Color Coding

Progress bars are color-coded by completion

Orange for low, yellow for mid, green near completion. This came directly from user feedback and carries through consistently to the partner retailer status cards in the tracking dashboard — keeping the visual language coherent across the product.

Tracking responses at scale

One screen replaced a daily status spreadsheet

This is where Flow earned its name. Opening any Flow shows the current status of every partner retailer it's been sent to, in one glance.

Add screenshot here — Coca-Cola Flow 2023 tracking dashboard showing partner retailer status cards (Kroger, Kroger + Criteo, 7-Eleven) with 3-step journey

Each card represents one retailer and shows three steps of their journey through the evaluation:

Insight Sent to Partner Admin — the RFI has been emailed and accessed. Scoring Complete / Ready for Scoring / Submitted for Review — the current state of the response. Approved — the GroupM admin has signed off and the data is ready to publish.

The contextual CTA on each card changes with state. A retailer mid-scoring shows "Score" as the active action. An approved retailer shows "Review." A retailer still in early stages has the action greyed out — there's nothing to do yet.

Once every retailer is approved, the Publish Flow button becomes active. That's the moment the cycle officially closes and the data flows back into Fusion for Coca-Cola's media planners.

For the commerce team, this screen replaced a status spreadsheet that had to be manually updated every day. For GroupM admins running multiple Flows in parallel, it was the difference between "I'll get back to you" and "I can tell you exactly where we are."
Iterating from user feedback

Tags → Progress bars: a small change with large impact

The first version of the Flow homepage used tags to indicate status — small pills that read "In Progress," "Awaiting Review," "Complete." It tested well enough to ship in early prototypes.

It didn't hold up with real users.

Once the commerce team started using the prototype with actual workloads, the feedback was consistent: tags told them what state a Flow was in, but not how close it was to being done. They were used to thinking in proportions — "we've got responses from 30 out of 50 retailers" — and a discrete tag didn't capture that.

✓ Shipped in MVP

The change was small in design effort and large in user impact. It's also a useful reminder that the right answer from user research isn't always to add features — sometimes it's to swap the affordance you already have for one that maps better to how users think.

Outcomes & impact

Flow's MVP shipped on time — and landed on three fronts

Weeks → Days
RFI cycle time reduced. A complete Coca-Cola Retail Capabilities Evaluation — built, sent, collected, scored, approved, and published — went from a multi-week process to a few days.
1 → Multiple
Brands adopting Flow. Coca-Cola was the launch client. Once the MVP validated the model, additional brands were already in onboarding when our team merged with Copilot.
4 → 40+
Team members after merger. Our four-person team was folded into Copilot — a 40+ person org — as the platform was recognized as foundational to GroupM's broader commerce product strategy.

The architecture of Flow + RFIs was deliberately built to be reused. Coca-Cola was the launch client, but once the MVP proved out, expansion to additional brands required no redesign — a direct outcome of the early decision to abstract RFIs as templates and Flows as instances.

Reflections

What this project taught me

What I learned

The most valuable lesson from Flow was that the right metaphor matters more than the right component library. We had Material UI components available for every part of the product. But the moment that made the RFI Builder work was choosing to treat the form like a spreadsheet — not because spreadsheets are pretty, but because that's the mental model the commerce team already had from years of doing the work in Excel.

Designing for the mental model, not the technology, was the through-line of the whole project. Working at the speed of a four-person team also taught me how to make tradeoffs in real time. When there's no buffer between design and engineering, every decision is also a feasibility conversation. I left this project a stronger collaborator with engineers than I went in.

What I'd do with more time

With more runway I would have invested earlier in the retailer-facing experience. Partner retailers were our second-most-important user, but they got less attention in the MVP than GroupM admins did. A smoother retailer onboarding flow would have reduced the support load that came with each new client cycle.

I would also have built in-app analytics for the commerce team showing patterns across Flows — which retailers consistently respond slowest, which questions get the most freeform "Other" responses, where admins spend the most time. Closing that feedback loop would have made every future RFI better than the last.
