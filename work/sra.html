The challenge

Designing a home for a place that didn't exist yet.

The Ashram is a new physical destination — no photography, no established digital presence, no existing brand for the U.S. audience. The website had to introduce the space, communicate its history and offerings, and support fundraising, all before the sanctuary had opened.

The core tension: how do you make people feel something about a place they can't yet visit? The answer had to come from content strategy, visual atmosphere, and storytelling — not photography.

On top of that, the design had to honor the heritage of the mother Ashram in India while feeling modern and accessible to a U.S. audience, and serve both first-time visitors and existing members at the same time.

"The most challenging brief is designing for a vision rather than a reality — you're not documenting something that exists, you're creating belief in something that will."

Audience

Six audiences. One coherent experience.

Audience mapping surfaced six distinct visitor types — each with different motivations, questions, and success criteria:

First-time visitors
Spiritual seekers
Current members
Retreat & program participants
Yoga, meditation & sound healing visitors
Donors & supporters

Rather than building six separate journeys, I designed a layered entry point system — the homepage directs each persona toward their path within the first scroll, without feeling like a branching menu. The goal: every visitor lands and immediately feels "I want to come here."

UX approach

Three principles that guided every page.

01

Evoke before inform

Feeling before facts. Every page leads with atmosphere and emotion before moving into practical information.

02

Reduce friction to giving

The donation flow was designed to be as few steps as possible. Every extra click is a donation lost.

03

Earn trust through detail

Specifics about the land, the mission, and the people behind it build credibility for a place visitors haven't seen yet.

Content-heavy pages like Our Story and Social Impact were the hardest to design. Dense history and philosophy had to be shaped into experiences that felt scannable and emotionally engaging — not overwhelming. I used section pacing, visual pauses, quotes, and layered hierarchy to make rich content feel like something worth reading.

Visual design

A palette drawn from the land itself.

With no photography available, the visual identity had to carry the atmosphere on its own. The palette was drawn from the Poconos landscape — saffron, flame, auburn, and alabaster — warm, earthy, and alive. The goal was for users to feel the place before seeing it.

Typography used STIX Two Text for body — a scholarly serif with warmth — paired with a clean geometric sans for navigation and labels. Traditional enough to honor the Ashram's roots, modern enough to welcome a U.S. audience.

Quotes from the spiritual founder became a recurring design element across pages — placed to create emotional depth and reflection without over-explaining the philosophy. In some areas, quotes were paired with audio for a more immersive, sensory moment.

Reflections

What I took from this project.

Content design is UX

This project made clearer than any other that content strategy and UX are the same discipline. IA, copy briefs, and page layouts were inseparable — you couldn't design one without the other.

Vision-first design

Designing for something that doesn't exist yet forced intentionality on every decision. There was no reality to fall back on — just the story we were telling and whether it landed.

Donation flow deserves disproportionate attention

The giving experience is where mission meets action. Every friction point is a donation lost. I spent more time on this flow than any single page and it was absolutely worth it.

Design for evolution

The template system was built knowing photography would eventually arrive. Planning for that shift from the start meant it won't require a redesign — just a content update.
