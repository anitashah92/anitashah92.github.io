The challenge

Designing a home for a place that didn't exist yet.

The Ashram is a new physical destination — no photography, no established digital presence, no existing brand for the U.S. audience. The website had to introduce the space, communicate its history, and support fundraising before the sanctuary had even opened.

The core tension: how do you make people feel something about a place they can't yet visit? Content strategy, visual atmosphere, and storytelling had to do the work that photography normally would.

The design also had to honor the heritage of the mother Ashram in India while feeling modern and accessible to a U.S. audience — serving first-time visitors and existing members simultaneously.

"The most challenging brief is designing for a vision rather than a reality — you're not documenting something that exists, you're creating belief in something that will."

Audience

Six audiences. One coherent experience.

Audience mapping surfaced six distinct visitor types. Rather than building separate journeys, I designed a layered entry point system — the homepage directs each persona toward their path within the first scroll.

UX approach

Three principles that guided every page.

01

Evoke before inform

Feeling before facts. Every page leads with atmosphere and emotion before moving into practical information.

02

Reduce friction to giving

The donation flow was designed in as few steps as possible. Every extra click is a donation lost.

03

Earn trust through detail

Specifics about the land, the mission, and the people behind it build credibility for a place visitors haven't seen yet.

Content-heavy pages like Our Story and Social Impact were the hardest to design. Dense history had to feel scannable and emotionally engaging — I used section pacing, visual pauses, quotes, and layered hierarchy to make rich content feel worth reading.

Visual design

A palette drawn from the land itself.

With no photography available, the visual identity had to carry the atmosphere alone. The palette was drawn from the Poconos landscape — saffron, flame, auburn, and alabaster — warm, earthy, and alive.

Typography paired a scholarly serif with warmth for body text alongside a clean geometric sans for navigation. Traditional enough to honor the Ashram's roots, modern enough to welcome a U.S. audience.

Quotes from the spiritual founder became a recurring design element — creating emotional depth without over-explaining. In some areas, quotes were paired with audio for a more immersive, sensory moment.

Reflections

What I took from this project.

Content design is UX

This project made clearer than any other that content strategy and UX are the same discipline. IA, copy briefs, and page layouts were inseparable — you couldn't design one without the other.

Vision-first design

Designing for something that doesn't exist forced intentionality on every decision. There was no reality to fall back on — just the story we were telling, and whether it landed.

Donation flow deserves the most attention

The giving experience is where mission meets action. Every friction point is a donation lost. I spent more time on this flow than any single page — and it was worth it.

Design for evolution

The template system was built knowing photography would eventually arrive. Planning for that shift from the start meant it won't require a redesign — just a content update.
