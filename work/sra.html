Overview

A digital home for a place still coming to life

Shrimad Rajchandra Ashram, USA is an upcoming wellness sanctuary in the Poconos — a space designed for reflection, learning, community, and inner growth. Because the Ashram is a new physical destination, there was no existing website or digital presence for the space.

The organization needed a website that could do several things at once: introduce the Ashram to people who had never heard of it, communicate its history and spiritual roots, support visitor planning, and lay a scalable foundation for future growth. The first launch includes 16 pages spanning core storytelling, visitor information, programs, activities, and practical planning content.

As Lead Designer, I owned this project from strategy through QA — including information architecture, wireframes, visual design, content-design alignment, development handoff, and ongoing build review. I also co-owned the IA with the content team and worked directly with the development agency to keep the final build aligned with the intended experience.

The core challenge: designing a website for a place that didn't yet exist, that needed to feel real, beautiful, and worth visiting — before a single guest had walked through the door.
The challenge

Inspiring and credible, before the doors open

The hardest part of this project was that the Ashram couldn't speak for itself yet. There were no guest reviews, no photos of packed retreat halls, no word-of-mouth to lean on. The website had to help people understand not only what the space offers, but why it exists, what it feels like, and how they could engage with it — entirely through design.

At the same time, the project carried a set of competing demands that had to be balanced carefully:

Audience

First-timers and existing members

The site needed to serve people discovering the Ashram for the first time and longtime members of the community — two very different needs in the same experience.

Heritage

India roots, U.S. audience

The U.S. Ashram is connected to a mother organization in India. The design needed to honor that heritage without feeling inaccessible to a Western wellness audience.

Scale

16 pages, built to grow

The launch scope was large, and the site was designed with the expectation that it would continue to expand. Every pattern had to work both now and later.

The website could not simply be informational. It needed to feel immersive, welcoming, and emotionally connected to the physical space — so that when someone landed on the homepage, their reaction was: I want to come here.

Design direction

Modern, spiritual, and grounded in place

The visual direction needed to balance heritage and accessibility. Because the U.S. Ashram is connected to a mother organization in India, we inherited parts of an existing visual and spiritual identity. At the same time, the new website needed to feel relevant, polished, and approachable for a U.S. audience.

I honored the traditional cues of the broader organization while introducing a more modern, spacious digital experience. The brand palette — anchored in saffron, flame, auburn, and alabaster — informed the color direction across the site, paired with STIX Two Text for headings and Inter for body copy.

UX approach

Three principles that shaped every page

Principle 01

Storytelling as structure

Rather than treating each page as a static information container, I designed the site to guide users through the Ashram's space, history, culture, and offerings in a more immersive way. The storytelling shows up through page structure, section pacing, photography, pull quotes, audio moments, and a layered content hierarchy that moves between emotional and practical information. The goal was for users to feel like they were beginning to experience the Ashram before they'd physically arrived.

Principle 02

Balancing heritage and accessibility

The Ashram has deep roots in its mother organization in India. The design needed to honor that spiritual heritage without feeling traditional in a way that would feel inaccessible to a U.S. wellness audience. I kept select traditional elements — symbolism, warmth, spiritual tonality — but paired them with clearer layouts, stronger hierarchy, and modern content structures. The result is a design that feels rooted, but not closed.

Principle 03

Making content-heavy pages digestible

Some of the most challenging pages — Social Impact, Our Story, Inspiration & Founder — needed to communicate history, philosophy, and organizational context without overwhelming users. I broke dense content into clear sections, created strong visual hierarchy, used storytelling beats to create flow, balanced text with imagery, and built in visual pauses between heavier content areas. The goal was not just to place content on the page, but to make the content feel meaningful and easy to move through.

Key design decisions

Four decisions that defined the experience

Decision 01

Immersive first impression

For the homepage and key storytelling pages, the design uses full imagery, spacious layouts, and thoughtful pacing to create a sense of arrival. The user should feel they've stepped into a place — not landed on a page.

Decision 02

Quotes as a design system

Quotes from the spiritual guru became a repeating design element across the site. Rather than over-explaining the philosophy, these moments let the site create emotional depth and reflection — and in some areas, were paired with audio for a more sensory experience.

Decision 03

Program pages as individual experiences

Each offering — yoga, meditation, sound healing, courses — has its own dedicated page, but they're built from a shared system. Users understand each activity clearly while still feeling the coherence of the larger Ashram experience.

Decision 04

Designing for scale from day one

The site structure, page templates, and design patterns were created to support future content, additional programs, events, and visitor resources. Every pattern decision was made with the question: will this still work when the site doubles in size? The first launch is the beginning of a larger digital ecosystem, not the end state.

Content collaboration

Design and content developed together

For a storytelling-heavy website, the experience only works when structure, visuals, and messaging are developed in parallel. I worked closely with the content team throughout the project — not just reviewing pages after the copy was written, but shaping the content direction alongside them.

Together, we worked through questions that sat at the intersection of content and UX:

→What does a first-time visitor need to understand first? Helping sequence information so users weren't asked to understand the spiritual lineage before they understood what the space was.
→Where does the story need more emotion? Identifying moments where the content was functional but cold, and finding ways to bring warmth back.
→Where does the content feel too heavy? Flagging sections where dense paragraphs were burying the experience, and finding visual or structural ways to lighten the load.
→What should be shown visually instead of explained in text? Pushing toward photography, visual hierarchy, and spatial design when words alone weren't doing the job.
→How do we keep the experience welcoming for both new and existing audiences? Ensuring pages served returning members without alienating newcomers, and vice versa.

This content-design partnership was especially important for the three most complex pages: Our Story, Social Impact, and Inspiration & Founder — all of which had significant historical and spiritual weight to carry.

My focus was on protecting the overall experience rather than chasing pixel perfection — reviewing through the lens of hierarchy, responsiveness, consistency, and usability.

Reflections

What leading this project taught me

What I learned

This project challenged me to think beyond individual page design and consider the full digital experience of a new physical destination. Because the Ashram is both spiritual and practical, the website needed to hold multiple layers at once — inspiring but not vague, informational but not overwhelming, rooted in tradition but still modern and accessible.

The most important lesson was the value of aligning content and design early. For a storytelling-heavy website, the experience only works when structure, visuals, and messaging are developed together. Trying to design around unresolved content — or letting content be written without design context — creates gaps that are very hard to fix later.

What I'm proud of

I'm proud that this project gave me the opportunity to lead across strategy, UX, visual design, content collaboration, and development QA. Working at that breadth — from early IA decisions through final build review — gave me a clear view of how each phase connects to and affects the others.

I'm also proud of how the design navigated the tension between heritage and accessibility. The Ashram's roots are specific and meaningful, and honoring them while making the site genuinely welcoming for a new U.S. audience required care and intentionality at every step.
