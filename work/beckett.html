The problem

It's January. The house is cold. Nobody knows why.

Homeowners with oil-heated homes had no reliable way to know their fuel level without physically checking the tank — often outside, often in winter. Beckett's IoT sensor could transmit real-time tank data, but there was no consumer app to surface it clearly.

The design challenge was translating a single data point — tank fill percentage — into something genuinely useful: when to order, how urgent it is, and what to do when something goes wrong.

"IoT design lives or dies on edge cases. A tank reading of 0% could mean empty, or it could mean the sensor is offline. Those states need very different responses."

Research

Understanding the mental model of "fuel."

User interviews with homeowners surfaced a consistent mental model: fuel is thought of like a gas tank — people want a simple visual gauge, not raw percentages. They also think in terms of days of fuel remaining, not gallons or percentages.

Research also revealed the high-anxiety moments: unexpected cold snaps, extended absences from home, and the anxiety of not knowing if a delivery arrived correctly. Every design decision was filtered through these scenarios.

Design

Preference testing the gauge.

Four display formats were tested with users to find the most intuitive fuel gauge representation.

Dial gauge

Familiar, but imprecise

Users recognised it immediately from car dashboards. But they found it hard to read exact levels and felt it took up too much space.

Vertical bar

Clear and scannable

Simple fill bar with colour shift (green → amber → red) as level drops. Users found it the fastest to read at a glance. Paired with "~14 days remaining" text below.

Testing

Where it got hard: the edge cases.

The core flow was straightforward. The real design work was in the states that happen when things go wrong — and making sure users could tell the difference between a real problem and a false alarm.

Low fuel alert — triggered at 20%, with a direct "Order fuel" CTA linking to the homeowner's preferred supplier. Tested timing of the notification: too early caused alert fatigue, too late caused anxiety.

Sensor offline — distinct visual state making clear the reading may be stale. Users needed to know the app wasn't broken — just the connection. Added a "Last updated X hours ago" timestamp.

Post-delivery confirmation — after an oil delivery, the gauge should update. If it doesn't within 24 hours, prompt the homeowner to verify the delivery happened correctly.

Reflections

What I took from this project.

Design for the worst day

The happiest state — full tank, sensor connected — needed almost no design. The real work was in low fuel, offline, and error states. That's where trust is made or broken.

Preference testing is worth it

Four gauge options felt like overkill early on. But the clear preference for the vertical bar — and the reasons behind it — shaped the whole visual language of the app.

Consumer vs. enterprise

Coming from enterprise tools, this project was a shift. Consumer users have zero tolerance for confusion and no training budget. Every label and icon had to stand alone.

What I'd add

A delivery scheduling integration — so users could reorder directly from the app rather than being sent to a third-party site. The handoff felt abrupt in testing.
