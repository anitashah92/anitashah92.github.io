The problem

Designing a home for a place that didn't exist yet.

Stone Ridge Audubon was a sanctuary in early development — no photography, no established brand, no existing web presence. The website had to convey the vision of a place, attract donors and visitors, and serve as a fundraising tool, all before the physical space was complete.

The core tension: how do you make people feel something about somewhere they can't yet visit? The answer was rooted in content strategy — language, atmosphere, and storytelling had to do the work that photography normally would.

"The most challenging brief is designing for a vision rather than a reality — you're not documenting something that exists, you're creating belief in something that will."

Strategy

Six audiences. One experience.

Audience mapping identified six distinct visitor types: major donors, general donors, retreat guests, day visitors, local community members, and press/media. Each had different motivations, questions, and success criteria.

Rather than building six separate journeys, I designed a layered entry point system — the homepage directs each persona toward their path within the first scroll, without making it feel like a branching menu.

Three UX principles guided every page decision: evoke before inform (feeling before facts), reduce friction to giving (donation flow in as few steps as possible), and earn trust through detail (specifics about the land, the mission, and the people behind it).

Content & IA

16 pages. Every word intentional.

Working closely with the SRA founders, I developed the information architecture across five site sections: About the Place, Programs & Retreats, Conservation, Give, and Community.

Content design involved writing briefs for each page — defining the emotional job (what should a visitor feel?), the informational job (what do they need to know?), and the action job (what should they do next?). This gave the copy collaborators a clear framework rather than an open canvas.

Visual design

A palette drawn from the land itself.

With no photography available, the visual identity had to carry the atmosphere. The palette was drawn directly from the Poconos landscape: saffron, flame, auburn, and alabaster — warm, earthy, alive.

Typography used STIX Two Text for body copy — a scholarly serif with warmth — paired with a clean geometric sans for navigation and labels. The combination felt like a place that took nature seriously without being austere.

Reflections

What I took from this project.

Vision-first design

Designing for something that doesn't exist yet forced me to be more intentional about every visual and copy decision. There was no reality to fall back on — just the story we were telling.

Content strategy is UX

This project made clearer than any other that content design and UX are the same discipline. The IA, the copy briefs, and the page layouts were inseparable.

Donation flow deserves more attention

The giving experience is where mission meets action. Every extra step or moment of confusion is a donation lost. I spent disproportionate time here and it was worth it.

What's next

Once photography becomes available, the visual design will shift significantly — images should do the atmospheric work currently carried by colour and type. Planning for that evolution was baked in from the start.
