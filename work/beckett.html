The problem

It's January. The house is cold.

You check the thermostat — it's fine. You head to the basement and find the oil tank sitting empty. Your supplier won't be out until tomorrow.

For Beckett Corporation customers, this wasn't a rare edge case. It was a recurring reality.

Beckett has been building oil and gas burners since 1937 — reliable, industrial hardware trusted by homeowners and commercial customers alike. But their devices had no way to communicate. Tanks ran dry without warning. Suppliers were called reactively. Customers were left in the cold — literally.

Their vision was to change that. By adding IoT sensors and WiFi-enabled hubs to their existing hardware, Beckett wanted to give users real-time visibility into their tanks from anywhere in the world. My job was to design the mobile experience that would make that possible.

My role

Sole UX Designer on a cross-functional team

I joined as the sole UX Designer and Consultant on a small cross-functional team. I owned the end-to-end design process — from initial research and user interviews through wireframing, hi-fi mockups, preference testing, and a final clickable prototype.

Working directly alongside engineers from day one meant design decisions were constantly pressure-tested against technical feasibility — which shaped the final product in meaningful ways.

Discovery & research

Three inputs that shaped the entire product

Before sketching a single screen, I needed to understand what users actually cared about — and what they didn't. Research came from three distinct sources.

01 — User surveys

What do users actually want to see?

Surveys surfaced three non-negotiable alert states: low tank levels, low transmitter battery, and maximum fuel usage. Crucially, users didn't think in terms of "sensors" — they thought about their tank. We abstracted the sensor layer entirely, surfacing only the physical object the user cared about.

02 — User interviews & A/B testing

How should the app behave?

Interviews added texture: users wanted a seamless at-a-glance level view, real-time updates from anywhere, push notifications the moment levels dropped, and per-device notification control — not a one-size-fits-all toggle.

03 — Industry specialists

What do users not know to ask for?

Consulting safety engineers at Beckett surfaced a use case users wouldn't have thought to request: freeze alarm alerts. When ambient temperatures drop too low, oil can't be stored safely. Domain expertise filled a gap that user research alone couldn't.

Defining the system

What to hide is as important as what to show

Before jumping into flows or wireframes, I mapped the hardware into three distinct objects — and made a deliberate decision about how much technical complexity to expose to the user.

The key design decision here wasn't visual — it was conceptual. By deciding what to hide as much as what to show, the app stayed approachable for everyday homeowners while still giving power users access to technical details when they needed them.

User flows

Mapping the journeys before designing the screens

Rather than designing screens in isolation, I mapped the key journeys first — ensuring the architecture made sense before any visual decisions were made. Two flows defined the core experience.

The first covers onboarding: a new user lands, signs up with email, and arrives at their dashboard. A returning user follows the same path but with a sign-in shortcut and password recovery fallback.

The second — and more complex — flow covers device setup. From the dashboard, a user can add either a hub or a tank sensor. Each path has its own configuration steps: hubs require WiFi setup, tank sensors require shape and size selection. Both resolve back to an updated dashboard with the new device visible.

Visual design & branding

Making a heritage brand feel at home on iOS

Beckett is a conservative, established brand — and the visual design needed to honor that while still feeling modern and trustworthy on a mobile device. I worked within their existing brand guidelines: bold red as the primary color, black, and white, with Times New Roman for serif contexts and Arial/Helvetica as sans-serif alternatives.

The challenge was making a heritage brand feel at home in an iOS app without stripping away its identity. The solution was restraint: red was reserved for the navigation bar, key CTAs, and critical alerts — carrying weight and urgency only where it mattered most. The rest of the UI stayed clean and neutral, letting the data breathe.

Red is a powerful color. Use it everywhere and it loses meaning. Reserve it for moments that actually need it.
Preference testing & iterations

Design decisions validated with real users

Design decisions weren't made by gut — they were validated with users through preference testing on UsabilityHub. Three key decisions went through this process.

The fuel gauge

I tested a traditional analog dial versus a color-coded arc gauge. Users preferred a hybrid — the familiarity of a dial combined with urgency-coded color. The final design used a circular dial with a red-to-green gradient arc to communicate level at a glance.

Option 1 — Analog dial

Traditional needle gauge, E to F. Familiar, like a car's fuel gauge. No color encoding.

Option 2 — Color arc

Modern color-coded arc from red to green. Visually expressive, immediately communicates urgency.

✓ Informed final design

Hub signal display

A simple icon with percentage label versus a side-by-side bar chart showing gauge and hub signal independently. Users preferred the bar chart — it gave them granular information without feeling cluttered.

Option 1 — Single bar

Combined signal strength as one progress bar with percentage. Simple but loses device-level detail.

Option 2 — Split bar chart

Gauge and Hub signal shown side by side. Users could see which specific device had the issue.

✓ Selected by users

Hub icon

A WiFi funnel icon versus a clean signal bar chart. Users preferred the bar chart — the funnel icon wasn't immediately recognizable as a hub.

Option 1 — Funnel icon

WiFi funnel shape with signal bars. Technically accurate but unfamiliar to non-technical users.

Option 2 — Signal bars

Familiar cellular-style signal bars. Universally understood metaphor for connectivity strength.

✓ Selected by users

Design decisions

Three choices worth calling out

These decisions are worth highlighting because they each show how research directly shaped the UI — not the other way around.

01 — Graphs

Fill history vs. usage history

The tank detail screen surfaces two distinct graphs rather than one combined view. This came directly from user interviews: people wanted to understand both when they refilled and how fast they were burning through oil. Separating them made each dataset easier to read and act on.

02 — Alerts

Per-device notification control

Notification settings live at the device level, not as a global toggle. Users with multiple devices in different locations wanted granular control. The freeze alarm toggle on hub settings came specifically from industry specialists — a reminder that domain expertise fills gaps user research alone cannot.

03 — Color

Red nav bar vs. grey nav bar

A deliberate decision was made to use dark grey for internal screens and reserve the red bar for top-level branded screens only. This prevented red from feeling repetitive or alarming in non-critical contexts, while preserving its visual weight for moments that actually needed it.

Reflections

What this project taught me

What I learned

The most valuable lesson from Beckett was learning to design at the intersection of three competing forces: what users want, what the client is willing to accept, and what the engineering team can actually build.

When I originally designed the device setup flow it was a multi-screen step-by-step process — thorough, user-friendly, and expensive to build. Working closely with engineers, I redesigned it to repurpose the existing settings page for setup, cutting the screen count significantly without sacrificing the experience. That kind of pragmatism is only possible when you're embedded in the team from day one.

The client's conservative nature was also a real design constraint. Not every research finding made it into the final product — and learning to advocate for users while respecting a client's vision was a skill I built on this project.

What I'd do with more time

I would have designed a FAQ and in-app help section to reduce support load for Beckett's team.

I would have built out screens showing exactly how push notifications appear on the device's lock screen — a gap in the handoff documentation.

I would have designed a direct in-app channel for users to reach Beckett's support team, reducing the friction of finding a phone number or email address when something goes wrong.
