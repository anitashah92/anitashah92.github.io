The problem

Gigabytes of retail data. Zero way to compare it.

GroupM's commerce team was sitting on a massive amount of retail data with no easy way to compare it. For every brand they worked with — including Coca-Cola, Nestlé, and Unilever — evaluation data came in from multiple retail partners and channels, including CTV, search, display, social, and audio, spread across tools like Comscore, SimilarWeb, Criteo, and CitrusAd.

As a result, strategists relied on older software and PowerPoint to manually build charts for client presentations. Comparing performance across brands, retailers, or channels was inconsistent and time-consuming. Teams were spending hours assembling reports instead of doing analysis. Clients received static snapshots instead of live, interactive data.

Answering a simple but important question — how does Walmart compare to Kroger across our commerce channels? — often meant building a new slide deck from scratch. Insights was designed to solve that.

"The strongest design decisions aren't always the ones that go unchallenged — they're the ones you can defend with evidence after being challenged."

My role

The project I pushed hardest for.

Insights was the project I pushed hardest for and learned the most from. I was the sole designer throughout — from the first stakeholder session through to user testing and engineering handoff — under real constraints: a tight timeline, a small team, and data visualization territory that was genuinely new to me.

I owned requirements gathering, charting library research, wireframing, hi-fi design, prototype testing via Maze, and cross-functional negotiation with engineers on chart feasibility.

The fact that Insights ended up being demoed in client pitches for Unilever, and that the commerce team embraced it, made it the work I'm most proud of from my time at GroupM.

Discovery

Understand the data before designing the charts.

Before sketching a single screen, I needed to understand three things: what data the commerce team was working with, what questions they were trying to answer, and what our charting library could actually support.

01

Stakeholders

Requirements from the commerce team

I ran working sessions with stakeholders from the commerce team and engineers together. Two requirements shaped everything: retailers needed to be segmented into fair comparison groups (pharmacy vs. grocery vs. general), and the dashboard needed charts that could surface both overall performance and channel-level detail.

02

Data audit

Understanding the metrics

I worked with the commerce team to map every data metric that Flow would pipe into Insights: overall retailer scores, per-channel weights across nine channels (General, On-Property Search, On-Property Display, Off-Property Display, Social, CTV, Audio, Digital OOH, API), monthly traffic, revenue, and growth rate. The chart types had to follow from the data, not the other way around.

03

Charting library

Confirming what engineering could build

I aligned with engineers early on the charting library, Nivo Rocks, because our design system didn't include all the chart types we needed. Knowing the library's constraints before wireframing meant I wasn't designing charts that would be impossible to implement.

How Insights works

Flow feeds Insights — the two platforms work in tandem.

Insights didn't exist in isolation. The data powering every chart came from Flow — specifically from completed RFI evaluations that had been scored, approved, and published by the commerce team. Every insight was grounded in real retailer response data, not manually entered figures.

Setting up a new Insight was a three-step process in Admin Mode. Click each step to explore.

01

Name

Give the Insight a name

Name the Insight to map to the evaluation — e.g. "Coca-Cola USA Retail Media Capabilities." This keeps dashboards organized across multiple brand clients.
02

Attach Existing Flow

Connect a completed Flow

Connect a completed, published Flow — this is the data source that powers the dashboard. All scores, channel weights, and retailer data flow in automatically from here.
03

Weigh Segments

Set channel weights per segment

Set the channel weights for each retailer segment. Different segments — General, Grocery, Pharmacy, Pure Pay — can have different channel weightings, reflecting that a grocery retailer and a pharmacy retailer shouldn't be scored identically across all nine channels. This made scores more meaningful and client-defensible.

Each project also had its own access control — admins could manage who had edit access on a per-project basis, keeping Coca-Cola data siloed from Nestlé without any manual gatekeeping.

The dashboard

Four charts. Four analytical questions. One page.

The Insights dashboard was built around a specific insight about how the commerce team worked: they didn't have one question about their retailers — they had four. Each chart was designed to answer exactly one of those questions. Click each chart to expand.

Chart 01

Treemap

Who is leading and who is lagging across partner retailers?

+
Add treemap screenshot

Each retailer appears as a tile sized by overall score and colored on a dark-to-light green scale. At a glance, the commerce team could see exactly who was leading and who was lagging across all partner retailers.

This chart almost didn't ship. During hi-fi design, engineers raised a concern that lower-performing retailers could appear nearly invisible. I revisited the charting options and consulted a UX colleague — and came to the same conclusion: no other chart communicated ranked distribution across 15+ retailers as clearly as a treemap. The smaller tiles weren't a flaw; they accurately reflected each retailer's smaller footprint in the data. The treemap shipped.

Chart 02

Radar → Bubble Heatmap

How do specific retailers compare across channels?

+
Add heatmap screenshot

The treemap showed overall performance — but the commerce team's next question was always more nuanced: how does Amazon compare to Target across specific channels? A retailer strong in CTV but weak in On-Property Search tells a very different story than their overall score suggests.

I originally designed a radar chart: nine axes, each retailer plotted as a filled polygon. The max-3 retailer constraint was intentional — four or more overlapping polygons become unreadable. But user testing surfaced a critical problem: strategists regularly needed to compare five or more retailers simultaneously.

So I went back to research and landed on a bubble heatmap — a grid where retailers are rows, channels are columns, and each cell is a bubble sized and colored by score intensity. Bigger and darker bubbles mean stronger performance. Hovering any bubble highlights the entire row and column while greying everything else, letting users focus on one intersection without losing context. The commerce team's reaction when they saw it was immediate.

Chart 03

Bar Chart

How does traffic and revenue track month over month?

+
Add bar chart screenshot

Monthly traffic and revenue trends for each retailer, surfaced in a grouped bar chart. This gave strategists a temporal view to complement the snapshot-based treemap and heatmap — answering not just where retailers stand, but how they've been moving.

Chart 04

Retailer Maturity Scatter Plot

Where should a brand invest next?

+
Add scatter plot screenshot

A scatter plot mapping Retailer Score (current maturity) on the Y-axis against Average Growth Rate on the X-axis. Retailers fall into four quadrants telling a clear strategic story:

High score + high growth → priority partners. High growth + lower score → emerging opportunities. High score + low growth → established but potentially plateauing. Low on both → deprioritized.

Selected retailers from a brand's evaluation were highlighted in orange. Hovering any dot reveals the retailer's name and exact coordinates. Quadrant lines were set at the median, so clustering was always relative to the current dataset rather than a fixed benchmark. This chart ended up front and center in Unilever client pitches — it translated raw scores into business strategy in a way that raw numbers couldn't.

User testing

Maze prototype testing with the commerce team.

Testing was conducted with the primary users of the platform via Maze — covering the full admin flow (creating an Insight, attaching a Flow, configuring segments and weights) as well as the dashboard experience (reading and interacting with each chart).

The radar-to-heatmap pivot was the most significant thing testing surfaced, but not the only one. Testing also validated that commerce strategists needed the ability to create and name their own segments rather than working with a fixed taxonomy. The Manage Segments modal was a direct result — allowing admins to add, rename, and delete segments without engineering involvement.

Using Maze meant testing could happen asynchronously. Prototype clicks, completion rates, and qualitative comments gave us a structured way to prioritize what to revisit before handoff.

Reflections

What this project taught me.

Data viz is a research discipline

Every chart decision started with a question — what is the user actually trying to understand? Working backwards from that, not from aesthetics, saved enormous time and avoided charts that looked good but answered the wrong question.

Defend decisions with evidence

The treemap pushback wasn't comfortable. Going back with research and a peer consultation rather than capitulating was the right call. That experience gave me a framework I've used in every design conversation since.

What I'd add with more time

A mobile-responsive version and a presentation mode — full-screen, no UI chrome, chart-first. The commerce team frequently presented from laptops in client meetings, and the dashboard wasn't optimized for that context.

Flow + Insights together

Insights proved the value of the broader Fusion platform. Flow collected the data. Insights made it meaningful. Together, they gave GroupM something that hadn't existed: a single source of truth for retail media partner evaluation.
