The problem

It's January. The house is cold. Nobody knows why.

Homeowners with oil-heated homes had no reliable way to know their fuel level without physically checking the tank — often outside, in winter. Beckett's IoT sensor could transmit real-time tank data, but there was no consumer app to surface it clearly.

The design challenge: translate a single data point — tank fill percentage — into something genuinely useful. When to order, how urgent it is, and what to do when something goes wrong.

"IoT design lives or dies on edge cases. A tank reading of 0% could mean empty — or it could mean the sensor is offline. Those two states need very different responses."

Research

Understanding the mental model of "fuel."

User interviews surfaced a consistent mental model: fuel is thought of like a car's gas tank. People want a simple visual gauge, not raw percentages. They think in terms of days of fuel remaining, not gallons.

Research revealed the high-anxiety moments: unexpected cold snaps, extended time away from home, and not knowing if a delivery actually arrived. Every design decision was filtered through these three scenarios.

Design

Preference testing the gauge — four formats, one winner.

Four display formats were tested with users to find the most intuitive fuel gauge.

Dial gauge

Familiar, but imprecise

Users recognised it from car dashboards — but found it hard to read exact levels and felt it took up too much screen space.

Vertical bar

Clear and immediately scannable

Simple fill bar shifting green → amber → red as level drops. Fastest to read at a glance, paired with "~14 days remaining" below.

Edge cases

The real design work — when things go wrong.

The happy state — full tank, sensor connected — needed almost no design. The real work was in the states that happen when things don't go to plan.

🔴

Low fuel alert — triggered at 20%

Direct "Order fuel" CTA surfaces immediately. Timing was critical — too early caused alert fatigue, too late caused anxiety. Testing found 20% was the sweet spot.

📡

Sensor offline — distinct visual state

A "Last updated X hours ago" timestamp makes it clear the reading may be stale. Users could distinguish between a real problem and a dropped connection without panicking.

✅

Post-delivery confirmation

If the gauge doesn't update within 24 hours of a delivery, the app prompts the homeowner to verify the delivery happened correctly — closing the loop on a common anxiety moment.

Reflections

What I took from this project.

Design for the worst day

The happy state needed almost no design. The real work was in low fuel, offline, and error states — that's where trust is made or broken.

Preference testing is worth it

Four gauge options felt like overkill early on. But the clear preference for the vertical bar — and the reasons behind it — shaped the whole visual language of the app.

Consumer vs. enterprise

Coming from enterprise tools, this was a shift. Consumer users have zero tolerance for confusion and no training budget. Every label and icon had to stand alone.

What I'd add

A delivery scheduling integration — so users could reorder directly from the app rather than being sent to a third-party site. The handoff felt abrupt in testing.
